Identity and Power

Identity

A figure showing a split individual. It represents the loss of individuality as a result of harsh social norms and rules.

The facet of identity plays a crucial role in determining a person's role in society. Social norm, however, restricts this level of control and imposes crude, stringent ideals that negatively affect an individual's growth. This can be seen in The Handmaid's Tale, where Offred's identity is stripped and shed into a role akin to that of a slave, essentially. In Boys and Girls, the narrator is forced to pursue a persona of being a "girl", by her environment.

Oppression

An image displaying a broken chain, symbolizing oppression and loss of self-decision.

Oppression is directly linked to social and political systems that influence, govern, and enforce behaviors, and marignalize those who are outliers. This is prominently seen in The Handmaid's Tale, where compatible women are forcefully enslaved and oppressed to produce children, without any say. Similarly, in Aunt Jennifer's Tigers, Aunt Jennifer's marriage to "Uncle" exposes the hidden notions related to freedom of expression and control.

Expression

A needle and a thread representing the creation of art during times of suppression.

The ability to freely express thought, belief, idea, or a notion is a pivotal aspect to a person's quality of life. In the The Handmaid's Tale, Offred is forced to express herself through thoughts, eventual conversations with her Commander, and storytelling (with the imaginary reader). This idea of expression is emphasized in Aunt Jennifer's Tigers where Aunt Jennifer conveys her desire for liberty by producing symbolistic embroidery related to tigers.

Autonomy

This icon displays a locked padlock, symbolizing the freezing of autonomy under tyrannical powers.

Autonomy, or self-sovereignty, is often constricted under despots or totalitarian regimes in order to limit unity and proliferate collective control. In The Handmaid's Tale, Offred (along with most other women) have little to no say in their physical, emotional, or spiritual liberties. Additionally, Aunt Jennifer's Tigers also holds crucial elements regarding the loss of autonomy felt by Aunt Jennifer, as seen by the expression of her art relating to tigers, following her marriage.
